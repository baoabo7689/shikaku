Shikaku [Rectangles]
The Rules
	Match the Number: Every rectangle must contain exactly one number, 
	and the area of the rectangle (in grid cells) must equal that number.

	Fill the Grid: Every single cell on the grid must belong to a rectangle.
	No Overlaps: Rectangles cannot overlap or leave empty gaps.
	Shape Limits: Regions must be perfect rectangles or squares.


Solving Techniques
	1. Start with Prime Numbers
	Numbers that are prime (like 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13) 
	only have one possible rectangular dimension: 1 × N. 
	For example, a 5 must be a straight line of 5 cells. 
	This makes them highly restrictive and usually the best place to start.

	2. Evaluate Corners and Edges
	A corner clue is trapped by the grid's boundaries, severely limiting its possible shapes. 
	Look at clues nestled in corners and calculate if their allowed shapes can fit without clipping out-of-bounds.

	3. Large Numbers Partition the Grid
	Large clues in a grid leave limited room for other rectangles. 
	You can often test the 1 × N or N × M configurations for large numbers to see which ones break or box in other adjacent clues.

	4. Forced Shapes and Unreached Cells
	Scan the grid for isolated cells. 
	If an empty cell can only be reached by one specific number 
	(because all other neighbors are blocked or too far away), 
	you can instantly map out the dimensions of that number's rectangle.

	5. The Process of Elimination
	Because the entire grid must be filled without overlaps, 
	drawing one rectangle rules out those cells for all other numbers. 
	As the grid fills up, the remaining unassigned spaces will force the final shapes to lock into place.
